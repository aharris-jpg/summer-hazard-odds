Summer Hazard Odds

We analyzed four of the most common summer hazards , including shark attacks, lightning, jellyfish, and heat illness, across 42 of America’s most popular vacation destinations. Using historical data from the CDC, NOAA, and the International Shark Attack File, we calculated the implied probability of an incident for each hazard at each destination, based on a standard two-week summer trip with active outdoor activity.

The result is a data-backed risk index that puts each destination’s danger in real context These are not scare statistics. They are honest odds you can compare to things that happen in everyday life.

Key Findings

What the data reveals

Shark
New Smyrna Beach ranks #1 nationally
Volusia County, FL has recorded more unprovoked attacks than any other stretch of coastline in the world. Your shark risk there is 7x the US national average . Still just 0.00015% per trip, but the highest in the dataset.
Lightning
Rocky Mountain NP outranks every beach
Colorado’s exposed high-altitude terrain gives Rocky Mountain NP a higher lightning risk score than any coastal destination. Florida’s 79 CDC-recorded deaths 2006–2021 lead all states . Summer afternoon storms in the Rockies are a serious and underappreciated hazard.
Jellyfish
Hawaii’s box jellyfish arrive on a lunar calendar
Waikiki and the Florida Keys both hit the maximum jellyfish exposure score. Hawaii’s box jellyfish surge predictably 9–10 days after each full moon. Florida deals with Portuguese man-of-war year-round. Jellyfish risk runs as high as 0.8% per trip at peak destinations.
Heat
Grand Canyon hits 2.9%, the highest risk in the dataset
Arizona and Nevada score maximum on heat. A 2.9% chance of heat illness per 2-week trip isn’t a scare number . That is CDC data. Heat is the only hazard where the risk is genuinely comparable to everyday life events like a fender bender or a bee sting.

Methodology

What the percentages mean. Risk figures represent implied probability of an incident per 2-week summer trip with active outdoor activity. They are exposure models built from historical data, not actuarial probabilities or medical predictions.

Shark. ISAF historical attack counts divided by estimated annual coastal visitor volumes. Florida baseline: ~15 attacks per year against ~100 million coastal visits.

Lightning. NOAA state-level casualty rates per million people per year, adjusted for summer seasonality (74% of annual casualties occur June–August per NOAA SR-193) and scaled to a 2-week exposure window.

Jellyfish. NOAA exposure tier classifications for each destination. Tiers reflect species presence, seasonal activity, and reported sting frequency.

Heat. CDC Heat & Health Tracker tier ratings, anchored to Arizona’s documented 2.9% heat illness ER visit rate in peak summer months.

Overall tier. The average of applicable hazards only. Landlocked destinations score zero on shark and jellyfish, their overall reflects only the hazards that genuinely apply. Data retrieved May 2026. Not a scientific forecast.
